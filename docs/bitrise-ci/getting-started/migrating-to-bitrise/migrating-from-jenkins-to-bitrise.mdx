If you have been looking to switch from [Jenkins](https://www.jenkins.io/) to <NT>Bitrise</NT> to speed up your mobile CI/CD flow and ease dedicated manpower, look no further. This guide introduces the most important <NT>Bitrise</NT> features and how you can quickly settle into <NT>Bitrise</NT> from your <NT>Jenkins</NT> world.


## Why bother migrating?

<NT>Jenkins</NT> is a self-hosted CI server where you have to manually install and maintain most of the functionalities before you could run any build. It also requires dedicated engineering resources for regular maintenance. When you switch to <NT>Bitrise</NT>, you get to experience the comfort of using a cloud-based, mobile first CI/CD platform which offers out-of-the box functionalities for all your mobile development needs on one platform. This means there is:

- No need to download anything.
- No need for any on-premise servers or plugins.
- No need to worry about infrastructure, tools and virtualization.

<NT>Bitrise</NT> takes care of all of the above. We have a vast array of automatized <NT><GlossTerm baseform="step">Step</GlossTerm></NT>s, [API](/en/bitrise-ci/api/api-overview), [CLI](/en/bitrise-ci/bitrise-cli/installing-and-updating-the-bitrise-cli), and [up-to-date Stacks](https://stacks.bitrise.io) with a highly intuitive GUI, called <NT><GlossTerm baseform="workflow editor">Workflow Editor</GlossTerm></NT>, all available at your fingertips.

Learn more about our security measures on our [Security page](https://bitrise.io/platform/devops/security) which includes details on product, data, application, network, physical and business security.

Check out the world of automated mobile development with <NT>Bitrise</NT>!

## Quick start guide

This Quick Start Guide helps you start your first build on <NT>Bitrise</NT> with minimum config.

1. [Sign up for Bitrise](/en/bitrise-platform/getting-started/signing-up-for-bitrise) if you don’t have an account yet.
1. [Connect a repo](/en/bitrise-platform/getting-started/getting-started-with-the-bitrise-platform) and run an automatically configured standard <NT><GlossTerm baseform="workflow">Workflow</GlossTerm></NT> on any project.
1. Once you’ve looked through the generated YML, make the changes you need: if you’re opting for a custom code, pop it into our [Script Step](https://www.bitrise.io/integrations/steps/script), and run your first build. Or find the <NT>Steps</NT> in our [Step Library](https://www.github.com/bitrise-io/bitrise-steplib) to replicate your Jenkinsfile’s behaviors. You can also run builds locally on your computer by installing our [Bitrise CLI](/en/bitrise-ci/bitrise-cli/installing-and-updating-the-bitrise-cli), <NT>Bitrise</NT>'s open source runner.
1. After getting to your new <NT>Workflow</NT>’s first green build, set up other jobs by configuring [automatic triggers](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/build-triggers/configuring-build-triggers) or [scheduled builds](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/starting-builds/scheduling-builds).
1. Optimize your <NT>Workflows</NT> with [key-based caching](/en/bitrise-ci/dependencies-and-caching/key-based-caching/using-key-based-caching) for faster, safer, and more reproducible builds.
1. Need help? [Contact us](https://support.bitrise.io/hc/en-us).

If you are interested in the main <NT>Jenkins</NT>-<NT>Bitrise</NT> differences and how <NT>Bitrise</NT> enhance your mobile development process, then continue with the guide below.


## Managing builds on <NT>Jenkins</NT> and on <NT>Bitrise</NT>

A build on <NT>Bitrise</NT> is the process specified by the app’s [Workflow](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/workflows/workflows-overview). It is a series of [Steps](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/steps/steps-overview), defined in a <NT>Workflow</NT>, executed by the [Bitrise CLI](/en/bitrise-ci/bitrise-cli/installing-and-updating-the-bitrise-cli) on a clean virtual machine or locally on your machine. You can check your app’s builds on the <NT>Bitrise</NT> **<NT>Dashboard</NT>** or you can analyze your [build logs](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/build-data-and-troubleshooting/build-logs) on your app’s own **<NT>Builds</NT>** page.

### Triggering builds on <NT>Jenkins</NT> and <NT>Bitrise</NT>

In this section we describe how you can trigger builds on <NT>Bitrise</NT>:

- The **<NT>Build</NT> <NT>Now</NT>** function on <NT>Jenkins</NT> corresponds with [starting a build manually](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/starting-builds/starting-builds-manually): click the button on your builds page and either simply start a new build or tinker away with the [Advanced configuration options](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/starting-builds/scheduling-builds#advanced-configuration-options-for-startingscheduling-builds).
- The **<NT>Build</NT> periodically** function of <NT>Jenkins</NT> is the [Scheduling builds](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/starting-builds/scheduling-builds) function on <NT>Bitrise</NT>. A big advantage of <NT>Bitrise</NT> is that you don’t have to set up a `cron` job, like you would on <NT>Jenkins</NT>, to schedule a specific time. Instead, you can use a visual scheduler to pick the days and time, or enter a `cron` expression directly if you prefer.
- For any Git related events, such as code push, pull requests, and Git tags, you can [configure triggers](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/build-triggers/configuring-build-triggers) that automatically start a build on <NT>Bitrise</NT>.
- The **<NT>Build</NT> after other projects** function of <NT>Jenkins</NT> is equivalent to the [chaining Workflows together](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/workflows/managing-workflows) on <NT>Bitrise</NT> where the <NT>Workflows</NT> run in succession. It’s surprisingly easy to chain <NT>Workflows</NT> together on <NT>Bitrise</NT>.
- You can trigger builds by any other remote system: use [Webhooks overview](/en/bitrise-platform/integrations/webhooks/webhooks-overview). We’re integrated with <NT>GitHub</NT>, <NT>Gitlab</NT>, <NT>Bitbucket</NT>, Gogs, <NT>Slack</NT>, Visual Studio, Assembla, and Deveo.
- You can also [push back build status reports](/en/bitrise-ci/configure-builds/configuring-build-settings/reporting-the-build-status-to-your-git-hosting-provider) to your Git provider (<NT>GitHub</NT>/<NT>GitLab</NT>/<NT>Bitbucket</NT>).

### <NT>Environment Variables</NT> and <NT>Secrets</NT> on <NT>Bitrise</NT>

[Environment Variables](/en/bitrise-ci/configure-builds/environment-variables) (a key and value pair) can be defined on app, <NT>Workflow</NT> and <NT>Steps</NT> level.

You can do lots of interesting things with Env Vars:

- [Expose them and reuse them in another Step.](/en/bitrise-ci/configure-builds/environment-variables#setting-and-managing-env-vars-during-a-build)
- Copy an Env Var to another key.
- Overwrite an Env Var.
- Parameterize a build by adding custom Env Vars to a build.
- <NT>Set up</NT> <NT>Workflow</NT>-specific Env Vars on the **<NT>Env Vars</NT>** tab to run <NT>Workflows</NT> with Env Vars that are only available for that particular <NT>Workflow</NT>.

<NT>Secrets</NT> are a type of <NT>Environment Variables</NT> but special ones. They hide sensitive information in an encrypted format so that your private input is not exposed in the build logs or in the `bitrise.yml`. Secret <NT>Environment Variables</NT>, or <NT>Secrets</NT> in short, can be set by adding a key and the variable in the **<NT>Secrets</NT>** tab of the <NT>Workflow Editor</NT>. This is again a built-in feature of <NT>Bitrise</NT> which ensures the following:

- Your secrets are not shown in the `bitrise.yml`.
- Your secrets are stored encrypted.
- You can prevent exposing secrets on the UI by making them protected.
- Note that anyone might be able to do a workaround and log the value of secrets with a pull request, thus we advise NOT to expose secrets in PRs.

### Build caching

Every build on <NT>Bitrise</NT> runs on a clean virtual machine but it does not mean you have to do everything from scratch - [you can cache contents of important files and preserve them between builds.](/en/bitrise-ci/dependencies-and-caching/dependencies-and-caching-overview)


## <NT>Jenkins</NT> plugins and <NT>Bitrise Steps</NT>

A [Step](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/steps/steps-overview) is a pre-defined task in <NT>Bitrise</NT> just like the <NT>Jenkins</NT> <NT>Steps</NT>, however, a <NT>Bitrise Step</NT> can be easily configured on our intuitive UI or you can even pull in your own <NT>Step</NT> from your repository. You can configure the inputs and parameters that define a <NT>Step</NT> task, and view/reuse the outputs a <NT>Step</NT> generates. Reusing the output means that another <NT>Step</NT> in the same <NT>Workflow</NT> can use it as the value of one of its inputs.

As being a continuous integration tool itself, <NT>Jenkins</NT> <NT>integrates with</NT> other services with the help of plugins. <NT>Jenkins</NT> users have to manage plugins in a centralized place, called **<NT>Manage</NT> plugins**, install them globally (on their <NT>Jenkins</NT> server), then use them in their specific projects. If <NT>Jenkins</NT> notifies of a newer plugin version, <NT>Jenkins</NT> admins have to doublecheck with their teams if they’re running any builds since updating a plugin requires the server to restart. With <NT>Jenkins</NT> plugins, reverting to an older version is not possible, since only the <NT>latest version</NT> is available. In <NT>Bitrise</NT>, however, you do not have to install any <NT>Steps</NT> first, because you can add or remove any <NT>Step</NT> in your <NT>Bitrise</NT> <NT>Workflow</NT> at any time as you wish and it won’t block any running builds of the project. You can always revert back to a previous <NT>Step</NT> version too if that’s what you need.

What’s more: When editing your <NT>Workflow</NT> in the **<NT>Workflow Editor</NT>**, you can easily search for a <NT>Step</NT> based on functionality or platform you are looking for in the **<NT>Step Library</NT>** and add it to your <NT>Workflow</NT> straightaway - there is no need to install them prior to setting up your <NT>Workflow</NT>.

You can [create your own custom Step](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/developing-your-own-bitrise-step/developing-a-new-step) too and store in a repository, then you can reference it by a URL in your <NT>Workflow</NT>.

:::tip[A <NT>Step</NT> jolly joker: our <NT>Script</NT> <NT>Step</NT>]

Do you have a [custom script](https://bitrise.io/integrations/steps/script) you’d like to run in your <NT>Workflow</NT>? Select the **Script** <NT>Step</NT> from the <NT>Step</NT> Library and add your custom code to the **<NT>Script content</NT>** input.

:::

Discover our <NT>Steps</NT> on our [Integrations](https://www.bitrise.io/integrations/steps) page or right when editing your <NT>Workflow</NT>.

Learn what else you can do with <NT>Steps</NT>:

- [Adding Steps to a Workflow](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/steps/adding-steps-to-a-workflow).
- [Step versions](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/steps/step-versions).
- [Enabling or disabling a Step conditionally](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/steps/enabling-or-disabling-a-step-conditionally).
- [Developing your own Bitrise Step](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/developing-your-own-bitrise-step/creating-your-own-bitrise-project-scanner).

:::note[Using fastlane in <NT>Jenkins</NT>?]

You can easily put your fastlane lanes to work on <NT>Bitrise</NT> too. All you have to do is add a <NT>Bitrise</NT>’s [**Fastlane**](https://www.bitrise.io/integrations/steps/fastlane) <NT>Step</NT> to your <NT>Workflow</NT> and add your lane name into the <NT>Step</NT>. Start a build and your lane will run on <NT>Bitrise</NT>.

:::


## <NT>Jenkins</NT> <NT>Pipeline</NT> - <NT>Bitrise</NT> <NT>Workflow</NT>

A <NT>Jenkins</NT> <NT>Pipeline</NT> is equivalent to a [Bitrise Workflow](/en/bitrise-ci/workflows-and-pipelines/workflows/workflows-overview), but the <NT>Bitrise</NT> <NT>Workflows</NT> are much easier to manage: you can quickly create new <NT>Workflows</NT> based on existing ones, chain multiple <NT>Workflows</NT> together in a single build, or delete <NT>Workflows</NT> that you no longer need.

A <NT>Bitrise</NT> **<NT>Workflow</NT>** is a series of <NT>Steps</NT>, such as test, code sign, build APK/ ipa and deploy. When you add an project to <NT>Bitrise</NT>, a primary <NT>Workflow</NT>, created based on the <NT><GlossTerm baseform="project scanner">project scanner</GlossTerm></NT>’s findings, gets kicked off/triggered automatically.

You can have several <NT>Workflows</NT> for a certain project. For example:

- For any PR events, create a <NT>Workflow</NT> that, once triggered, runs quickly and only executes basic tests such as smoke tests.
- For a meatier <NT>Workflow</NT>, that runs all your test, we recommend creating another <NT>Workflow</NT> and run it overnight or schedule it on 6/12/24 hour interval.

The **<NT>Workflow Editor</NT>** is the main place for configuring your <NT>Workflow</NT>. <NT>Jenkins</NT> **<NT>Stage</NT>s** are equal to multiple <NT>Bitrise</NT> <NT>Workflows</NT> chained together inside a main <NT>Workflow</NT>. On <NT>Bitrise</NT> the <NT>Workflow Editor</NT> helps you to chain <NT>Workflows</NT> like one would chain toy trains after each other.

The <NT>Workflow Editor</NT> has other powerful features built in to assist you with mobile development:

- [Code signing](/en/bitrise-ci/code-signing/ios-code-signing/creating-a-signed-ipa-for-xcode-projects): Upload certificates, provisioning profiles, and keystores into <NT>Bitrise</NT> and and use our [iOS](/en/bitrise-ci/code-signing/ios-code-signing/ios-code-signing) and [Android](/en/bitrise-ci/code-signing/android-code-signing/android-code-signing-using-the-android-sign-step) signing <NT>Steps</NT> to do all the <NT>code signing</NT> automatically for you before releasing an app to an app store.
- [Secrets](/en/bitrise-ci/configure-builds/secrets): check out your project's secret <NT>Environment Variables</NT> or add new ones.
- [Environment Variables](/en/bitrise-ci/configure-builds/environment-variables): there is no confusion of secrets and Env Vars in <NT>Bitrise</NT>. They are neatly organized into separate tabs so that you know where’s what. Add project Env Vars or <NT>Workflow</NT> specific Env Vars here. You can also reference <NT>Secrets</NT> as Env Vars with $.
- [Triggers](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/build-triggers/configuring-build-triggers): You can configure triggers: code push events, pull requests, or tags can all be set up to automatically start builds on <NT>Bitrise</NT>.
- [Stack](/en/bitrise-build-hub/infrastructure/build-stacks/about-build-stacks): Check out the default stack of your project, select a new one from a dropdown menu or select a specific stack for one of your <NT>Workflows</NT>.

### Adding a new app to <NT>Bitrise</NT>

What **<NT>New</NT> item** means in <NT>Jenkins</NT> is the **adding a <NT>new project</NT> flow** on <NT>Bitrise</NT>, either [on the web UI](/en/bitrise-ci/getting-started/adding-a-new-project) or [from the CLI](/en/bitrise-ci/bitrise-cli/adding-a-new-project-from-a-cli). This is a highly automated flow where minimal configuration is needed from you and literally within a minute your new build starts. Our flow takes you through all the important phases: setting privacy, connecting your repository, setting up repository access, choosing a branch, configuring your project, setting up your build configuration and [configuring webhooks](/en/bitrise-platform/integrations/webhooks/adding-incoming-webhooks).

### Webhooks on <NT>Bitrise</NT>

<NT>Bitrise</NT> makes extensive use of webhooks, which you can enable with a simple click when connecting a project to <NT>Bitrise</NT>. It all makes sense to add the webhook during the initial setup and not having to search for this functionality when you try to quickly get your project to speed.

In short, there are two types of [Webhooks](/en/bitrise-ci/api/incoming-and-outgoing-webhooks):

- <NT>Incoming webhooks</NT>, registered with your Git service provider, are used to automatically trigger builds on <NT>Bitrise</NT>.
- <NT>Outgoing webhooks</NT> are used to send reports of build events to other services, such as <NT>Slack</NT>. They can be added either on the website or via the [API overview](/en/bitrise-ci/api/api-overview).


## Jenkinsfile - <NT>Bitrise</NT> YAML

A <NT>Jenkins</NT> <NT>Pipeline</NT> uses groovy code, while <NT>Bitrise</NT> uses the more highly structured YAML format. All <NT>Workflows</NT> are defined in the `bitrise.yml` file in YAML format, which you can edit by clicking the **<NT><GlossTerm baseform="bitrise.yml">bitrise.yml</GlossTerm></NT>** tab of the <NT>Workflow Editor</NT>.

You can store the `bitrise.yml` file of your app either on [bitrise.io](http://bitrise.io/) or [in your repository](/en/bitrise-ci/configure-builds/configuration-yaml/storing-an-apps-configuration-yaml#storing-the-bitriseyml-file-in-your-repository). Don’t worry, your secrets (for example, credentials and IDs) are safe with us.

You can also download the current `bitrise.yml` configuration of your app and run it locally with the [Installing and updating the Bitrise CLI](/en/bitrise-ci/bitrise-cli/installing-and-updating-the-bitrise-cli).

### Jenkinsfile - <NT>Bitrise</NT> yml file comparison

If you have been using a Jenkinsfile (<NT>Pipeline</NT> project) in <NT>Jenkins</NT> and and considering to switch to <NT>Bitrise</NT>, you can easily match Jenkinsfile stages with <NT>Bitrise Steps</NT>. If you have been using the old-school Freestyle project, then you have to map your configuration’s settings with a matching <NT>Bitrise Step</NT> or a <NT>Bitrise</NT> function. For example, match the **<NT>Build</NT>** section of your Freestyle project with a <NT>Bitrise</NT> build <NT>Step</NT>, such as the [Android Build](https://app.bitrise.io/integrations/steps/android-build) <NT>Step</NT>, or map the **<NT>Build</NT> <NT>Trigger</NT>** section of <NT>Jenkins</NT> with the [Triggers](/en/bitrise-ci/run-and-analyze-builds/build-triggers/configuring-build-triggers) function of <NT>Bitrise</NT>. Make sure you check out our [Step Library](https://www.bitrise.io/integrations/steps) for more inspiration.

## Master and agent on <NT>Jenkins</NT> - <NT>Bitrise</NT> stack

A stack is the type of virtual machine we use to run your build. For example, for a native iOS app, the best stack is one of our <NT>Xcode</NT> stacks. <NT>Stacks</NT> come with all of the necessary tools pre-installed, and are regularly updated to make sure they will serve all of your needs. This way you don’t have to bother with maintaining master and agent instances or adding a node machine to your groovy code to run a build on a certain platform.

When you add your app to <NT>Bitrise</NT>, our project selector automatically detects the type of your project and based on its platform type, adds a default stack to it. Your first build will run on this stack, which is a virtual machine with all the required tools pre-installed on it. Should you wish to use another stack, you can simply select it from the **<NT>Default</NT> <NT>Stack</NT>** dropdown menu on the **<NT>Stacks & Machines</NT>** tab. If you click the **<NT>More</NT> information** link, you can see the preinstalled tools with their versions.

We support the latest <NT>Xcode</NT> version shortly after its official release.

In our [system reports](https://bitrise.io/stacks) you can check the installed tools and their versions on each stack.

Learn more about our [macOS stack update policy](/en/bitrise-build-hub/infrastructure/build-stacks/macos-stack-update-policy).

If you are interested in how to have a <NT>Bitrise</NT>-managed infrastructure on your <NT>AWS</NT> environment, check out our [guides](/en/bitrise-platform/infrastructure/bitrise-on-aws--cloud-controller/bitrise-on-aws-overview). You can also run <NT>Bitrise</NT> on your <NT>self-hosted infrastructure</NT> if you follow [this guide](/en/bitrise-platform/infrastructure/running-bitrise-builds-on-premise).


## Managing teams and roles in <NT>Jenkins</NT> and <NT>Bitrise</NT>

A <NT>Bitrise</NT> user is an individual with a <NT>Bitrise</NT> account and belongs to one or more <NT><GlossTerm baseform="workspace">workspace</GlossTerm></NT>/s. A <NT>workspace</NT> is a group of users who collaborate on projects. You can create multiple <NT>workspaces</NT> and a contributor can be invited to multiple <NT>workspaces</NT> by other <NT>Bitrise</NT> contributors. A contributor must be assigned to a project to be able to work on it.

Learn how you can [add contributors to a project](/en/bitrise-platform/projects/managing-user-access-to-a-project) on the web UI either by adding existing <NT>workspace</NT> members or inviting outside contributors.

Contributors can have different roles in <NT>workspaces</NT> that determine what they can do and cannot do:

- When it comes to <NT>Bitrise CI</NT>, we differentiate [Owner, Admin, Platform Engineer, Developer, Testers/QA](/en/bitrise-platform/projects/roles-and-permissions-for-bitrise-ci) roles
- When it comes to [Release Management](/en/release-management/configuring-connected-apps/release-management-roles-and-permissions), we differentiate <NT>workspace</NT>-level roles (<NT>Workspace</NT> Owner, <NT>Workspace</NT> Manager, Contributor and Viewer) and Release-level roles (Release Manager and App tester). Check out roles and permission tables on [Release management apps](/en/release-management/configuring-connected-apps/release-management-roles-and-permissions#roles-and-permissions-for-release-management-apps) and [build distribution and releases](/en/release-management/configuring-connected-apps/release-management-roles-and-permissions).

You need a <NT>workspace</NT> to have a [paid subscription plan](https://bitrise.io/pricing?utm_medium=paid_search&utm_source=google&utm_campaign=all_misc_signup_paid_search_all_2025-04-10&utm_content=branding_pricing_emea&utm_source=google&utm_medium=cpc&utm_campaign=&utm_term=bitrise%20pricing&gad_source=1&gad_campaignid=22492269029&gbraid=0AAAAADEBC46C3y8wDrPzZEypPjE85IZGF&gclid=Cj0KCQiAq7HIBhDoARIsAOATDxDmUOF1Nw6xSIWC_imk3x-MBAxQW3VVSarlhc14CuKm4jV2yT3UDxMaApjPEALw_wcB) on <NT>Bitrise</NT>. Each of your <NT>workspaces</NT> can have a different subscription plan which determines how many credits your <NT>workspace</NT>'s projects can use.

You can use [SAML SSO or SCIM](/en/bitrise-platform/accounts/saml-sso-in-bitrise/configuring-saml-sso-on-bitrise) to securely onboard or offboard an enterprise to your <NT>workspace</NT>.


## Integrated SSO management on <NT>Bitrise</NT>

While in <NT>Jenkins</NT> you have to install an authorization plugin for SSO management, in <NT>Bitrise</NT> it comes as an integral part of our [paid plans](https://bitrise.io/pricing). <NT>Workspace</NT> with such plans [can set up SAML SSO](/en/bitrise-platform/accounts/saml-sso-in-bitrise/configuring-saml-sso-on-bitrise) as their single gateway to their <NT>Workspace</NT> on <NT>Bitrise</NT>. No more remembering usernames and credentials! All the <NT>Workspace</NT> owner has to do is set up <NT>Bitrise</NT> as a SAML SSO app on the SAML SSO provider and invite <NT>Workspace</NT> members to the <NT>Bitrise</NT> Org. We have specific guides to the [different SAML SSO providers](/en/bitrise-platform/accounts/saml-sso-in-bitrise/configuring-saml-sso-on-bitrise) to guide you through the steps.
